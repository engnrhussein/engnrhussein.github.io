Process Overview

This document provides a detailed examination of a chemical process simulated in DWSIM for the production of synthesis gas (syngas) and its subsequent purification to yield high-purity hydrogen. Syngas—a critical mixture of hydrogen (H₂), carbon monoxide (CO), and trace other gases—is a foundational building block in the modern chemical industry. Its applications range from the synthesis of methanol and ammonia to the production of clean, synthetic fuels. The separation of hydrogen from this mixture is paramount, as pure hydrogen is a vital reactant in industrial processes like hydrocracking and hydrodesulfurization, and a promising clean energy carrier for a sustainable future. The following sections present the process flowsheet, detailed stream data from the simulation, and an in-depth analysis of the process performance.

Unit Operation Description

  • Reactors (PFR-1, PFR-2): These are modeled as Plug Flow Reactors where the steam-methane reforming (SMR) reaction occurs at elevated temperatures, converting methane and steam into syngas. PFR-1 operates at a very high temperature (1200 °C) to maximize conversion.
  • Heat Exchangers (HX-1, HX-2, etc.): A network of heat exchangers is used to recover heat from hot process streams and use it to preheat colder streams. This is critical for improving the energy efficiency and economics of the process. For example, E4 is a major heater supplying 23,962.03 kW, while the cooler for PFR-1 removes 19,370.52 kW.
  • Separators (FS-1, FS-2): These flash separator units are essential for separating vapor and liquid phases. FS-2, for instance, is crucial for separating the condensed water and other components from the hydrogen-rich gas stream.
  • Compressors & Pumps (C-1, C-2, PUMP-1): These mechanical units increase the pressure of gas and liquid streams to meet the requirements for reactions and separations. Compressor C-2 significantly increases the pressure of the final hydrogen product stream, consuming 1,260.81 kW of power.
  • Mixer (MIX-1): This unit combines the pre-heated methane feed with steam before it enters the reforming section.
  • Storage (TANK-1): Represents a storage vessel for liquid streams, likely water, within the process.

Process Performance Analysis

Conversion and Selectivity

The simulation demonstrates an effective conversion of methane feedstock into syngas. In Stream 7, leaving the initial high-temperature zone, the methane molar fraction is reduced to 0.02, with a high yield of hydrogen (0.71) and carbon monoxide (0.22). After further processing and reactions, the final product stream (Stream 29) contains a hydrogen molar fraction of 0.9982, indicating a highly successful purification process. The remaining impurities are extremely low, with methane at just ~13 ppm and water at ~52 ppm.

Energy Management and Integration

Energy integration is a cornerstone of this process's design. The high energy demand of the reformers is partially offset by a network of heat exchangers that recover and reuse thermal energy.

  • Major Energy Consumers: The primary energy input is Heater E4, which provides 23,962.03 kW to preheat the feed. The compressors are also significant power users, with C-1 requiring 155.95 kW and C-2 needing 1,260.81 kW to bring the hydrogen product to its final pressure of 700 bar.
  • Major Energy Recovery: Significant heat is recovered from the hot reactor effluent. The cooler associated with PFR-1 (E6) has a duty of -19,370.52 kW, and the cooler HX-2 (E8) removes another -2,400.50 kW. This recovered energy is essential for preheating boiler feed water and the reformer feed, thus reducing the overall operating cost.

Final Product State

The final hydrogen product in Stream 29 is delivered at a temperature of 15 °C and a very high pressure of 700 bar. The state is a dense, supercritical fluid, which is typical for hydrogen storage and transportation. The molar flow is 851.279 kmol/h, corresponding to a mass flow of 1716.08 kg/h.
